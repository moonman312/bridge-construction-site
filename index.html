Bridge and construction loans: structures and risks

What bridge debt is built for

Bridge loans are short-term, usually interest-only capital for transitional situations — acquiring, repositioning, or buying time to stabilize a hotel that doesn't yet support permanent financing. They close fast and underwrite your business plan rather than just trailing numbers. That flexibility is the upside; the trade-off is that you're borrowing against a future you still have to deliver.

The risks to weigh before you sign

Three risks dominate. Rate: most bridge debt floats, so payments rise with the index unless you buy a rate cap (which itself costs money to purchase and renew). Time: terms are short, often one to three years, so a slipped plan can hit maturity before the upside arrives. And takeout risk: the whole bet is refinancing or selling out of the bridge — if NOI lags or value falls, the exit may not clear the balance. Read the extension test, too; it usually requires hitting a DSCR or debt-yield hurdle plus a fee, exactly when it's hardest to qualify.

Construction adds its own

Ground-up and major-renovation loans layer on cost overruns, completion delays, draw timing, and an interest reserve that can run dry before the hotel opens and ramps. Completion guarantees are typically personal. Build in real contingency, and stress-test whether the stabilized NOI you're projecting can actually carry — and refinance out of — the debt you're taking on.

Questions, answered

What are the main risks of a bridge loan?
Three big ones: a floating rate that can rise, a short maturity, and takeout risk. You are betting on stabilizing and then refinancing or selling before it comes due — if rates rise, NOI lags, or value slips, the exit may not clear the balance.
Floating vs fixed bridge — which is riskier?
Floating is cheaper upfront but exposes you to rate increases; a rate cap limits that but costs money to buy and to renew. Fixed gives certainty but less flexibility and sometimes prepayment penalties. The right call depends on how long you will hold and your view on rates.
What is an extension test, and why does it matter?
Most bridge loans let you extend only if you hit a hurdle — usually a minimum DSCR or debt yield — plus a fee and often a fresh rate cap. Many borrowers fail the test exactly when they need the extension most, so read it before you count on it.
What is the risk specific to construction financing?
Cost overruns, completion delays, draw timing, and an interest reserve that can run dry before the hotel opens and ramps. Completion guarantees are usually personal — build in real contingency.
How do I know if I can carry a bridge loan?
Stress-test your projected stabilized NOI against the rate and the maturity date. The diagnostic estimates supportable debt and shows how your coverage moves if rates rise — a fast reality check before you commit.
